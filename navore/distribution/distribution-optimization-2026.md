# Distribution Optimization Plan 2026
**Navore Market — San Diego Operations**
*Version 0.2 · Updated: 2026-06-07 · Owner: Anthony Abusa, Founder*

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Distribution is the single largest variable cost in Navore's unit economics and the operational dependency for every LFPP grant milestone. Getting it right at the La Jolla hub before any expansion is not optional — it is the prerequisite.

**Where we are:** The La Jolla hub launched January 2026. Delivery and fulfillment processes are largely manual, no written SOPs exist, and delivery route optimization has not been implemented. Current hub capacity is estimated at 50–70 orders/day before throughput breaks down. **Current actual volume: ~5–15 orders/week** (founder: "a handful/week") — deeply pre-breakeven on every delivery model.

**The financial reality:** At sub-100 orders/week, route-based home delivery loses money on most orders (fully-loaded cost: $8–15/order against ~$7.50–10 Navore revenue/order). Consumer hub pickup is the only model that is margin-positive at current scale. The plan below is designed around that constraint.

**What this plan achieves:**
- Reduce cost-per-delivery by 25–35% within 12 weeks through pickup-first model and route standardization
- Achieve ≥95% on-time fulfillment rate to satisfy LFPP reliability milestone
- Establish a documented, replicable hub SOP ready for county expansion by Q4 2026
- Define the order volume trigger (150 orders/week) at which route delivery becomes economically viable

**CEO decision required:** Three items in this plan require explicit founder authorization before execution (Section 8).

### 2.2 Known Operational Gaps (from BN-2 bottleneck analysis)

These are the constraints that will block any expansion if not resolved first:

1. **No SOPs** — hub open/close, packing sequence, producer check-in, and consumer handoff are not standardized. This means consistency depends on who is working, not on the process.
2. **No delivery route optimization** — routes are not planned for efficiency; drivers navigate ad-hoc. This directly inflates cost-per-delivery.
3. **No real-time inventory** — producers update availability by phone. Hub does not know what's available until product arrives.
4. **No throughput data** — no systematic measurement of time-per-order, packing bottlenecks, or fulfillment rate by zone.

**Consequence:** These gaps are the reason cost-per-delivery is elevated and scale is capped. No amount of zone expansion or producer growth will improve unit economics until the hub process is efficient.

## 3. Unit Economics: The Financial Case for Pickup-First

This section provides the decision-grade numbers the CEO needs. All figures assume a $50 average order value (estimate) and a 15–20% Navore commission (confirm actual rate). **At current volume (~5–15 orders/week), every delivery model loses money — the question is which model creates the fastest path through the breakeven valley.**

### 3.1 Revenue Per Order

| Commission Rate | Avg Order Value | Navore Revenue/Order |
|---|---|---|
| 15% | $50 | $7.50 |
| 20% | $50 | $10.00 |
| 15% | $65 | $9.75 |
| 20% | $65 | $13.00 |

*The 15–20% range is industry-standard for local food aggregators. Any commission below 15% makes the economics below unworkable at early volume.*

### 3.2 Cost Per Order by Delivery Model

**SD-specific cost inputs (sourced from industry benchmarks, 2025–2026):**
- Driver labor: $25–30/hour (gig market rate, San Diego)
- Route throughput: 8–12 stops/hour (urban/suburban SD)
- Fuel: $0.60/mile
- Hub facility (community center / farmers market time-slot): $200–500/week
- Hub labor (sorting, check-in, consumer handoff): ~$80–120 for a 4-hour Saturday window

| Model | Direct Cost/Order | Hub Cost Allocation¹ | Total Cost/Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Consumer hub pickup | $0 direct | $3–6/order | **$3–6** | Best margin; requires consumer behavior change |
| Navore route delivery | $8–12/order | $3–6/order | **$11–18** | Viable only at density (see 3.3) |
| Third-party (DoorDash/gig) | $15–25/order | $0 | **$15–25** | Emergency/sparse-zone use only |

¹ Hub cost allocation assumes $280–600/week facility + labor, divided by weekly order volume.

### 3.3 Break-Even Analysis by Volume

The table below shows gross margin per order (revenue minus delivery cost only; excludes tech, payment processing, and overhead). **Green = profitable at this volume. Red = loss-making.**

| Weekly Orders | Revenue (15–20%) | Pickup Cost/Order | Route Cost/Order | Pickup Margin | Route Margin |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 25 | $7.50–10 | $8–14 | $17–26 | **-$4 to -$0.50** | **-$16 to -$9** |
| 50 | $7.50–10 | $6–10 | $14–21 | **-$2.50 to +$1** | **-$11 to -$7** |
| 100 | $7.50–10 | $4.80–7.80 | $13–19 | **-$0.30 to +$2** | **-$9 to -$5** |
| 150 | $7.50–10 | $3.90–6.60 | $12–18 | **+$0.90 to +$3** | **-$8 to -$3** |
| 200 | $7.50–10 | $3.40–5.90 | $11–17 | **+$1.60 to +$4** | **-$7 to -$2** |

**Key findings for CEO:**

1. **Hub pickup becomes margin-positive around 100–150 orders/week.** Below that, even pickup is a loss after hub overhead. The business needs to grow through this valley quickly.

2. **Route delivery is loss-making at any volume below 200 orders/week at a 15–20% take rate.** Every route-delivered order burns cash. This is not a scaling problem — it is a structural mismatch between Navore's take rate and SD driver costs.

3. **Implication:** Until Navore reaches 150+ orders/week OR raises its take rate, home delivery must be a premium add-on priced to recover cost. The $4–6 convenience fee in Priority 2 (Section 4) is not optional — it is necessary to avoid per-order losses.

4. **Good Eggs benchmark:** Scaled to 500+ orders/week per hub with a hub pickup model before route delivery became standard. Farmigo operated similarly at 300–400 orders/week. Navore should not expect route delivery to be margin-neutral until that volume range.

### 3.4 Cold Chain Requirements (California)

These are legally required, not optional. They affect hub infrastructure decisions.

| Product | Storage Requirement | Hub Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Fresh produce | ≤41°F | Refrigeration required at hub |
| Dairy / eggs | ≤41°F | Same refrigeration zone as produce |
| Meat / poultry | ≤41°F (unfrozen) / 0°F (frozen) | Separate cold storage; Navore must confirm capability before onboarding meat producers |
| Dry goods / processed | Ambient (cool, dry) | Ambient storage OK |

**Confirmed:** Neither the La Jolla Methodist Church hub nor the Mission Valley YMCA hub has refrigeration. Dairy and certain temperature-sensitive produce cannot be safely aggregated at either location. Any producer currently claiming refrigerated product delivery is an active compliance risk. **Action required before onboarding any dairy or meat producers:** install portable refrigeration units at the anchor hub or identify a cold storage partner.

## 4. Optimization Priorities

These are ordered by: (1) dependency — earlier priorities unlock later ones; and (2) financial impact — earlier priorities have the largest effect on unit economics. **Do not skip or reorder these without reviewing the dependency map.**

### Dependency Map

```
P1: Hub SOPs + producer standardization
        ↓
P2: Pickup-first campaign + pricing differential
        ↓  
P3: Route density threshold enforcement
        ↓
P4: Metrics loop + route optimization tooling
        ↓
P5: Spoke hub expansion
```

P1 is the gate. None of the later priorities function without documented, reliable hub operations.

---

### P1: Hub SOPs + Producer Delivery Standardization — *Do Now (Weeks 1–6)*

**Why first:** Every other optimization depends on a stable, documented hub process. SOPs are the foundation. Without them, routes cannot be optimized because the hub itself is the variable.

**Actions:**
1. Write and post hub SOPs: open/close checklist, producer check-in, packing sequence, consumer handoff, issue escalation (Week 1–2)
2. Time the current process: measure actual time per order from arrival to handoff (Week 1–2)
3. Standardize producer delivery format: Navore labels, crate/box standards, drop-off window (Sat 7–9am), cutoff Thursday 6pm (Week 2–4)
4. Assign all active producers to nearest hub only (Week 2–4)
5. Test run: 1 full Saturday cycle with ≥3 producers, time each step (Week 4–6)

**Owner:** Founder/Ops
**Success metric:** Written SOPs published + 100% of producers hitting standardized delivery window by Week 6

---

### P2: Pickup-First Model + Delivery Pricing Differential — *Weeks 4–12*

**Why second:** Once the hub is running reliably (P1 complete), this is the highest-impact financial lever. Moving orders from route delivery ($11–18/order) to pickup ($3–6/order) directly improves gross margin by $5–12 per order switched.

**Actions:**
1. Launch hub pickup windows: Saturday 9am–12pm, 2 hours minimum (can add a weekday window as volume grows)
2. Price home delivery as a premium: +$5 delivery fee on all route-delivered orders. This partially offsets the $8–12 route cost; without it, delivery orders are guaranteed losses.
3. Make pickup compelling: product samples, recipe cards, seasonal feature at pickup window
4. Messaging: "Pick up fresh" — emphasize product quality vs. delivery convenience
5. Track pickup vs. delivery ratio weekly; target ≥60% pickup within 60 days

**Owner:** Founder/Ops + Marketing
**Success metric:** ≥60% of orders via hub pickup within 60 days of launch
**Financial impact:** Switching 60% of 100 orders/week to pickup saves ~$400–700/week in delivery cost vs. all-route model

---

### P3: Zone Viability Threshold Enforcement — *Ongoing*

**Why third:** Expanding to underviable zones before the core model is profitable accelerates losses. This is a rule, not a project — enforce it at every expansion decision.

**Rule:** Do not launch delivery to a new zone until ALL of these are true:
- [ ] ≥15 active subscribers within a 5-mile radius
- [ ] ≥3 active producers with product for that zone
- [ ] Route density projects ≥8 stops/hour at expected order volume
- [ ] Hub or mobile stop identified and confirmed

**Proposed zone sequence:**

| Zone | Launch Trigger | Estimated Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| La Jolla (existing) | Already launched | Optimize, don't expand |
| Encinitas / Del Mar area | 15 subscribers confirmed | Q3 2026 |
| Hillcrest / North Park | 15 subscribers confirmed | Q3 2026 |
| East County (La Mesa) | Threshold met | Q4 2026 if on track |
| South Bay | Threshold met | Q1 2027 |

**Owner:** Founder/Ops (threshold gatekeeper)
**Note:** Zone expansion timeline is secondary to hub profitability. If La Jolla is not margin-positive at pickup model by Q4 2026, pause expansion and diagnose.

---

### P4: Route Optimization + Metrics Loop — *Month 2 onward*

**Why fourth:** Optimization tooling is only valuable once there is a stable process to optimize. Buying route software while the hub has no SOPs is waste.

**Actions:**
1. Implement delivery route planning — even manual Google Maps optimization is a step change from ad-hoc driving. Target: all routes planned 24h in advance.
2. Establish weekly metrics dashboard (even spreadsheet): on-time rate, cost/delivery, pickup %, route stops/hour
3. Monthly review: Director reviews metrics, adjusts routes and zone thresholds based on actuals
4. Evaluate route optimization software (OptimoRoute, Circuit, Route4Me) once weekly route volume exceeds 50 stops. Below that, the tool ROI doesn't justify the cost.

**Owner:** Founder/Tech + Founder/Ops
**Success metric:** Metrics being tracked weekly; route planning done 24h in advance by Month 3

| Metric | Target | Review Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| On-time delivery rate | ≥95% | Weekly |
| Cost per delivery (fully loaded) | ≤$6 pickup, ≤$14 route | Monthly |
| Route density | ≥8 stops/hour | Monthly |
| Hub utilization | 50–80% capacity | Monthly |
| Producer on-time to hub | ≥95% | Weekly |
| Consumer pickup rate | ≥60% | Monthly |

---

### P5: Spoke Hub Expansion — *Q4 2026 and beyond*

**Why fifth:** Spoke hubs expand geographic reach but require P1–P4 to be working before the model is worth replicating.

**Hub tier structure:**

| Tier | Role | Target Count | Example Locations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anchor Hub | Full aggregation + sorting + pickup | 2–3 | La Jolla (existing), Escondido, Hillcrest |
| Spoke Hub | Pickup-only, consumer-facing | 4–6 | Del Mar, Carlsbad, Encinitas, La Mesa, Chula Vista |
| Mobile Stop | Route-based stop, no fixed facility | As needed | Dense residential clusters, event locations |

**Hub location criteria:**
- Within 20 minutes of primary consumer cluster
- Climate-controlled space available (refrigeration required for produce/dairy)
- Minimum 4-hour weekly window
- Accessible to both producer vehicles and consumer foot traffic
- Candidate venues: farmers markets with storage, community centers, cold storage partners, church halls

**Preferred hub partnership model:** Negotiate a weekly time-slot agreement ($200–500/week) rather than a lease. Lower commitment, easier to exit if a zone underperforms.
